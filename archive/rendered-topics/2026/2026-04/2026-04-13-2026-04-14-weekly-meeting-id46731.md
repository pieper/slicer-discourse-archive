## Post #4 by @lassoan (2026-04-18 03:42 UTC)

<p>We could relax/automate extension approval process if we had these:</p>
<ul>
<li>Filtering of extension by tier. This will be available very soon. <a class="mention" href="/u/sunderlandkyl">@Sunderlandkyl</a> may give an update at the next meeting. It will allow us to hide experimental (tier&lt;3) extensions.</li>

I’ve implemented this by <a href="https://github.com/Slicer/Slicer/pull/9118">generating a Slicer build tree stub</a> that is sufficient for building Python packages using the standard CMake scripts. <p>I agree that we need to do something sustainable.</p>
<p>The current heavy process that is similar to app store approval process is just not sustainable without dedicated funding or developers paying the costs.</p>
<p>“Community review process” may not solve the problem automatically, as it suffers from the same issues as the broken scientific paper peer review process. It is now much easier to generate an extension or write a paper, but people don’t have more time to review other people’s stuff.</p>
<p>We could allow publishing without review and let extensions emerge by getting likes or star ratings and comments - similar to YouTube, github, etc. We could then review highly rated extensions to bring them up into a higher tier.</p>

## Post #9 by @lassoan (2026-04-18 14:40 UTC)

<p>Yes, sandboxing would be important. Users cannot trust extensions but - more importantly - they cannot trust AI agents.</p>
<p>App stores provide some sandboxing but each platform is completely different and it would be significant effort on each to get into a store (and extensions and Python packages would complicate things further).</p>
<p>So, probably containers would be the most economical solution. If someone does not have compatible hardware or too difficult to set up then running on cloud be a good option.</p>
<p>Web browser is a good sandbox, too, so as SlicerTrame, VTK wasm, pyodide are improving, we’ll be able to seriously use that, too (maybe in a couple of years).</p>

<p>The process is not broken, commercial publishers choose profit over the community service the reviewers provide. I reject every single review request from commercial publishers (unless it is a non-profit society journal published by a commerical publisher). I am sure Nature/Elsevier can afford to pay their reviewers a meaningful honorarium when they are charging $12,000 for open access fee for a single publication and raking couple billion on profits for the equivalent of providing the platform.</p>
<p>We do not need to  do the same. I brought this up a few times before. High demand extensions can pay an annual fee for the review and support. This is fair in the sense that they are also the ones who are using the infrastructure most. The question whether there is enough extension to support to make this viable (and not prohibitively costly) and in what way we can collect this money (Kitware?) and how do we distribute to the reviewers. I don’t know the answer to that.</p>
<p>As for the new extensions, perhaps an AI agent can review the code for malicious injections and we simply advertise them as “use at your own risk”?</p>
